§ 01 · Summary

The content is careful; the design asks the reader to take the author's word for it.

The review document is unusually disciplined in its argument: it distinguishes verdict categories, names its falsification conditions, and invites correction. Visually, though, it competes with the very thing it critiques — stacked oversized numerals, red alarm chrome, and dashboard fatigue — and in doing so borrows the rhetorical posture of the site it is reviewing. A debunking document's strongest credibility signal is restraint.

§ 03 · Findings

Nine findings, ordered by leverage

Each finding names a current pattern, explains the cost, and proposes a concrete change. Severity dots: structural, notable, polish.

01
Hierarchy

The overview page competes with itself.

Within the first viewport the reader meets a title, a subtitle, a version string, a date, a four-up set of oversized numeric statements (69 · 0 · 4+4 · "Wrong Side"), a "Latest Findings" news ticker, and a second four-up verdict breakdown — before the argument begins. Each element is styled as a hero. Collectively they flatten into noise: the reader cannot tell which number is the thesis and which is support.

A review document has one thing to say on arrival. On this site, the most important sentence — "none of the 69 predictions produces a dome-specific result confirmed by measurement" — is structurally equivalent to four filler statistics next to it.

RecommendationPromote one sentence to the hero. Demote every stat to a single row of small-cap figures beneath it. Move the news ticker below the fold.
02
Color · Tone

Red alarm chrome undermines the sober argument.

The document's text is careful — it acknowledges kernels of truth, concedes specific points to the author, and names conditions under which its own verdicts would flip. The color system does none of that work. Red "KILL SHOTS", "Wrong Side" banners, and saturated failure tallies signal a verdict before the reader has read a sentence.

For an audience already sympathetic, this reads as energy. For anyone undecided — which is the only audience that matters for a debunking document — it reads as rhetorical pre-commitment, the same posture the review accuses the original site of.

RecommendationRestrict red to actual contradictions inside tables. Use a neutral ink for section chrome. Let the words do the verdict work.
03
Evidence UX

The verdict taxonomy is a real contribution. Its visualization isn't.

Six verdict categories with crisp definitions — Refuted by Data, Self-Contradicted, Misleading, Std Model Explains, Not Demonstrated, Unfalsifiable — is the document's intellectual backbone. It gives a reader a way to audit the reviewer. But the categories appear first as four large numerals with uneven priority, then as a scatter of colored pills inside a long table. The distribution of verdicts (9, 14, 22, 18, 4, 4) is the single most compelling data object in the document and it is nowhere presented as a chart.

RecommendationA single ordered bar — categories sorted by severity, labeled, accessible — placed at the top of the review. Every WIN then links from its verdict chip back to the definition. See §R2.
04
Typography

One typeface is carrying the whole document.

A single sans is doing running text, display, numerals, tables, code, and UI chrome. The result is that long-form reading feels like navigating a dashboard. There is also no typographic distinction between the reviewer's voice, quoted formulas, and the author's claims — everything speaks at the same volume.

RecommendationThree roles: a serif for argumentative prose, a humanist sans for UI/metadata, a monospace reserved for formulas and data. Inline formulas like H(r) = 8,537 × exp(−r/8,619) should read as evidence, not as running text.
05
IA

Tab-based IA hides the argument.

Eleven top-level tabs (Overview, Evaluation Guide, The Model, Self-Contradictions, 69 Wins, Live Power, Kill Shots, Timestamp Error, Predictions, External Tests, AI & Conclusions, References) give every section equal top-level weight. In practice the reader has to infer the intended reading order, and the document's own Tier 1 / Tier 2 / Tier 3 structure — which is stated, in prose — is never expressed in the navigation.

RecommendationA single long-form scroll with a persistent left rail, three grouped tiers, and anchor links. Keep tabs only for appendices (References, AI Directives). See §R1.
06
Rhetoric

Accuracy-math callouts out-shout the evidence they support.

"Honest accuracy: 89.6%, not 94.5%" is an important finding, but it is presented at the same visual pitch as the thesis. Ratios derived from a disputed denominator carry less argumentative weight than a single self-contradiction from the model's own geometry — but the design treats them as equals. Readers leave remembering the percentage, not the geometry.

RecommendationReserve display-scale numerals for Tier 1 arguments (self-contradictions with no external data). Accuracy-math belongs in a small sidebar.
07
Framing

"Latest Findings" reads like a news site, not a review.

Dated news entries on a review document imply that the review is chasing the subject — each update a reaction to the author's latest version. That is structurally true but rhetorically weak: it tells the reader the document is reactive rather than foundational.

RecommendationKeep a changelog, but move it to the References page or a small footer. Reframe the top of the site around the stable argument, not the news.
08
A11y

Dense on desktop, denser on phone.

Verdicts are conveyed through color alone in several places; some backgrounds drop below WCAG AA against their text; long tables with sticky left columns become unusable at narrow widths; and tap targets for internal anchors are small. For a document whose whole premise is readers should verify this themselves, the friction to do so on a phone is high.

RecommendationPair every color with a glyph or label; re-specify the palette at AA against paper; collapse verdict tables into stacked cards on mobile; enlarge anchors to 44px minimum.
09
Trust

There is no visible way for the reader to be convinced.

The prose states "four conditions would change our verdict." This is one of the strongest trust signals a critical document can offer — and it is buried in running text. The visible surface shows tallies of failures; it does not show the path by which the document would update itself.

RecommendationA persistent "What would change our mind" module on the overview, with each condition, its current status, and a link to the evidence. This is both a credibility signal and an invitation — and costs nothing the prose doesn't already contain.

R3 · "What would change our verdict"

Current

Buried in the Evaluation Guide as four prose paragraphs

The document names four concrete conditions under which it would upgrade a verdict. This is arguably its single strongest credibility signal. Today it lives inside a tab the casual reader won't open.

evaluation guide · deep section

Condition 1: Derive a discriminating prediction from dome geometry. Use the model's own stated parameters — H(r) = 8,537 × exp(−r/8,619), disc radius 20,015 km, κ = 1.67 nT/μGal — to derive a quantitative prediction that differs from what standard physics predicts…

Condition 2: Produce a genuine prospective prediction. Register a specific quantitative prediction (value ± uncertainty, named observable, named measurement station) before the confirming data exists…

Condition 3: Derive 7.83 Hz from dome geometry.…

Condition 4: Produce a coordinate system that achieves sub-5% error without globe inputs.…

  • Visible only after the reader commits to a tab
  • No visible status on whether any condition is close
  • No link from overview stats back to this list

Persistent four-row module on the overview

Each condition is a row: short name, one-line description, and current status ("0 of 69 meets this", "1 partial attempt", etc.). The whole module is visible on first load and links into the evidence.

§ 05 · Principles

Five principles for a document that disagrees well

None of these require a rewrite — the text is already doing the work. The job for the design is to get out of the way.

1. Let restraint carry the verdict. A quiet page with a hard claim reads as confident. A loud page with the same claim reads as advocacy. The reviewer has the evidence; the design should trust it.

2. Separate argument from atmosphere. Tier 1 self-contradictions deserve display weight. Tallies, changelogs, and dashboards do not.

3. Treat the taxonomy as the primary artifact. The six verdict categories are the review's most transportable idea. They should be legible from any point in the document and reinforced wherever an individual WIN is referenced.

4. Give the reader the path to change your mind. A visible falsification module is worth more than a dozen failure tallies. It converts "we think they're wrong" into "here's the test."

5. Design for the phone first. The audience for a debunking document is not a desktop researcher — it is the person who just received a link. If the first viewport on a phone doesn't land the thesis, nothing else matters.

The goal is not to sound fair. It is to be legible enough that a careful reader can tell you are being fair. — Design note, for any review document
§ 06 · Caveats

What this review is and isn't

Scope: visual / structural design only Subject-matter dispute: not adjudicated Fidelity: illustrative, not pixel-perfect Sample: overview, verdict table, eval guide

This review takes no position on the underlying cosmological dispute. Its recommendations assume the author's argument is worth presenting well; they do not evaluate whether it is correct. The mocks above are structural sketches, not ready-to-ship layouts — use them as a brief, not a spec. If you decide to share or cite the original site, the design caveats in §01 are the ones most likely to affect how readers receive it on first contact.
